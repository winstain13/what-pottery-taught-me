What Pottery Taught Me

The beauty of having less control than you think

The Journey

1
Greenware

The fragile beginning—shaped by hand, full of potential, but still vulnerable to every touch and temperature change.

2
Bisqueware

Hardened by fire—no longer clay, but not yet complete. Porous and ready to receive what comes next.

3
Finished Product

Transformed through heat and time—glazed, fired, and finally whole. A collaboration between intention and the unpredictable.

Stage 1

Preparing the Clay

30 minutes – 1 hour

Before you can create, you must prepare. This stage is about getting the clay ready—removing air bubbles, achieving the right consistency, and making sure your material is workable. It's humble work, but essential.

Steps

  • Weighing the clay to the desired amount
    Measure out the right amount of clay for your intended piece. Too little and you won't have enough to work with; too much adds unnecessary weight and difficulty.
  • Wedging to remove air pockets and achieve consistency
    Knead the clay using a rocking motion to eliminate air bubbles that could cause cracks or explosions during firing. This also ensures uniform moisture throughout.
  • Kneading until the clay feels smooth and pliable
    Continue working the clay until it has a consistent texture with no hard or soft spots. Properly prepared clay is easier to center and shape on the wheel.
Stage 2

Forming (Wheel Throwing)

1 – 3 hours

This is where the vision begins to take shape. The wheel spins, your hands guide, and the clay responds. Centering is everything—without it, nothing holds. This stage is both meditative and demanding, requiring patience and focus.

Steps

  • Centering the clay on the wheel
    Apply pressure to align the clay perfectly on the wheel's axis. Without centering, the piece will wobble and be impossible to shape evenly.
  • Opening the center to create the initial form
    Push down into the centered clay to create the interior space. This determines the wall thickness and interior depth of your piece.
  • Pulling the walls upward to shape the vessel
    Use gentle, consistent pressure between your inside and outside hands to raise the walls. Each pull thins and lengthens the clay, gradually forming your desired height and shape.
  • Shaping and refining the form
    Adjust curves, flare the rim, or compress the lip to finalize the form. Small adjustments here have a big impact on the final aesthetic.
  • Removing the piece from the wheel
    Use a wire tool to cut the piece free from the wheel head, then carefully lift it onto a board to dry. Handle with care—it's still very soft and vulnerable.
Stage 3

Trimming

30 minutes – 1 hour

Pottery gets better when you remove what doesn't belong. You refine the shape, make it lighter and cleaner, and realize that the first version was never the final version. Sometimes making something beautiful means cutting away, not adding more.

Steps

  • Allowing the piece to dry to leather-hard stage
    Wait until the clay is firm enough to handle without deforming, but still soft enough to carve. This "leather-hard" stage is critical—too wet and it will collapse; too dry and it will crack.
  • Re-centering the piece on the wheel (upside down)
    Carefully position the piece upside down on the wheel and center it so you can work on the bottom. Precision here ensures even trimming.
  • Trimming excess clay from the bottom
    Use trimming tools to carve away extra weight from the base. This makes the piece lighter and reveals its true form, removing what throwing left behind.
  • Creating a foot or base
    Shape a foot ring to give the piece a defined resting point and add visual elegance. The foot is both functional and aesthetic—it completes the design.
  • Smoothing and refining edges
    Clean up any rough spots, refine curves, and ensure all surfaces are smooth. This is your last chance to perfect the form before it becomes permanent.
Stage 4

Drying

1 – 7 days

Patience is required. The piece must dry slowly and evenly to avoid cracks. This is when you step back and let time do its work. At this stage, the clay is called "greenware"—fragile and full of possibility.

Steps

  • Cover lightly to slow drying and prevent cracking
    Use plastic or light cloth to slow moisture evaporation. Uneven drying causes stress and cracks, so patience is essential. Thicker pieces need more time.
  • Allow to air dry completely (bone dry)
    Let all moisture evaporate naturally until the clay is pale and chalky. Any remaining water will turn to steam in the kiln and could cause the piece to explode.
  • Handle with extreme care—greenware is very fragile
    At this stage, the piece is at its most delicate. Even a light bump can chip or shatter it. Move carefully and respect the vulnerability of unfired clay.
Stage 5

Bisque Fire

12 – 24 hours (including cooling)

The first real surrender. Heat transforms the clay permanently—what was soft becomes hard, what was fragile becomes durable. Once it enters the kiln, part of the outcome belongs to the elements. Small assumptions get exposed.

Steps

  • Load greenware carefully into the kiln
    Arrange pieces so they don't touch and air can circulate. Greenware can touch during bisque firing (unlike glaze firing), but careful placement prevents breakage.
  • Fire to bisque temperature (around 1800–1900°F / 980–1040°C)
    Heat the kiln gradually to allow remaining moisture to escape and chemical changes to occur. The clay transforms permanently—molecular water is driven out and the clay hardens irreversibly.
  • Allow kiln to cool completely before opening
    Resist the urge to open early. Rapid temperature changes can crack your pieces. Cooling takes as long as firing—patience is mandatory.
  • Unload bisqueware—now porous and ready for glaze
    The bisque-fired piece is now durable enough to handle, but porous enough to absorb glaze. It's entered a new state—no longer clay, not yet ceramic.
Stage 6

Glazing

1 – 2 hours

You choose colors and finishes, layering intention onto the surface. You make your best guess about what the piece will become. Glaze is a promise, not a guarantee. The way it looks now is not how it will look after fire.

Steps

  • Clean bisqueware to remove dust
    Wipe or rinse off any dust or debris. Contaminants interfere with glaze adhesion and can create defects in the final surface.
  • Apply glaze by dipping, brushing, or spraying
    Layer liquid glaze onto the bisqueware. The dry, porous clay absorbs the water and the glaze particles adhere. Thickness matters—too thin and it won't melt properly; too thick and it can run or crack.
  • Wipe glaze off the bottom (foot) to prevent sticking
    Remove glaze from any surface that will touch the kiln shelf. Molten glaze is like glass—it will fuse your piece to the shelf permanently if left on the bottom.
  • Allow glaze to dry before loading into kiln
    Let the water evaporate so the glaze sets on the surface. Wet glaze is fragile and will smudge easily. Once dry, it's powdery and ready for fire.
Stage 7

Glaze Fire

12 – 24 hours (including cooling)

The transformation you can't fully watch. Heat, chemistry, timing, and atmosphere all matter. The kiln collaborates whether you want it to or not. This is the reveal—the moment when expectation meets reality, and the finished piece can exceed your plan or ignore it entirely.

Steps

  • Load glazed pieces into the kiln (spacing carefully)
    Keep pieces from touching—molten glaze will fuse them together. Use stilts or supports if needed. The arrangement affects airflow and even heating throughout the kiln.
  • Fire to glaze temperature (around 2200–2400°F / 1200–1300°C)
    Heat melts the glaze into a glassy coating, bonding it to the clay body. Chemical reactions occur, colors transform, and the final surface emerges. This is where surprises happen—the glaze you applied may look entirely different now.
  • Allow kiln to cool completely—sometimes overnight
    The hardest part: waiting. Opening too early risks thermal shock and cracking. The cooling process is as critical as the firing itself.
  • Unload finished pieces
    Open the kiln and meet your work for the first time. Colors have shifted, surfaces have glossed or matted, and the piece you imagined has become something real—and often, something unexpected.
Stage 8

Buffing & Finishing

15 – 30 minutes

The final care. Small finishing touches matter. You slow down enough to really see the piece. By now, it is no longer the thing you first imagined—and that is usually a good thing. You sand any rough spots and appreciate what emerged.

Steps

  • Sand any rough spots on the foot with sandpaper
    Smooth the unglazed bottom so it won't scratch surfaces. Use fine-grit sandpaper or a grinding stone to remove any sharp points left from kiln stilts or rough clay.
  • Inspect for glaze defects or sharp edges
    Check for pinholes, crawling, or drips that created sharp edges. Small imperfections are part of pottery's character, but safety and function matter too.
  • Clean and polish the finished piece
    Wipe away kiln dust and fingerprints. Some potters use a light polish to enhance the glaze's shine. This is the final touch before the piece enters the world.
  • Admire your work
    Take a moment to appreciate what you've created. It's no longer the clay you started with—it's transformed through your hands, time, fire, and a bit of luck into something entirely new.

What Pottery Taught Me

You never really know, from the moment you first form the piece to the moment it is finished, what it will truly look like. Control is always partial. The process is part collaboration, part surrender. Surprise is not failure. The final form carries more than the original plan. That's the beauty of pottery—it taught me how to enjoy the unexpected.
